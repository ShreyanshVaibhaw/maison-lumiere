Building the autumn tasting menu

How a single crate of late figs and a cold snap on the water reshaped the whole card.

Every autumn menu at Maison Lumière begins not at a desk but at the back door, where the first deliveries of the changing season land before the dining room has even woken up. This year it arrived as a wooden crate of late figs, still warm from the orchard, and a phone call from our diver warning of a cold snap pushing the scallops deeper. Within an hour the seven courses we had sketched in September were quietly torn up.

Letting the ingredient lead

We have a rule in this kitchen, learned the hard way over fifteen years: the best ingredient of the day decides the menu, not the other way around. Those figs were too good to relegate to a garnish. So the opening course became a simple plate of fig, cured ham, and a thread of aged balsamic - barely cooked, almost arrogant in its restraint. When something is that ripe, our job is mostly to stay out of its way.

The cold water changed the middle of the menu just as decisively. With scallops in short supply, we leaned into the root vegetables coming up from the farm: a velvety celeriac soup poured table-side, a roasted parsnip with brown butter and hazelnut, and a course built entirely around the season's first squash. The sea would return; for now, the soil carried the menu.

The shape of seven courses

A tasting menu is an argument with a beginning, a middle and an end. We open bright and acidic to wake the palate, build slowly through richer and warmer dishes, and resolve with something that lingers. This autumn, the arc moved from that cool fig opener through the earthy heart of root vegetables and slow-cooked duck, before landing on a dessert of roasted pear, brown sugar and a whisper of bay leaf from the terrace.

Sommelier Tomas Vance built the pairing in parallel, course by course, tasting alongside the kitchen each afternoon. A tense Loire white for the figs, a soft Rhône red as the dishes deepened, and a late-harvest pour to close. The wine is never an afterthought; it is written at the same time as the food, in the same pencil.

What stays, what changes

By the time the menu reached the printer it had been rewritten four times in three days, and it will keep shifting through the season as the figs give way to quince and the scallops return. That is not indecision; it is the whole point. A tasting menu that could be served in March has, in our view, missed the only thing worth saying in October.

If you would like to taste this autumn before it turns to winter, the seven courses are served Wednesday through Saturday. As always, tell us of any allergies when you book, and we will quietly rebuild a course or two around them.
