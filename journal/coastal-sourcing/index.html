Sourcing · 3 September 2025

The coastal sourcing we live by

Meet the divers, farmers and foragers whose mornings shape our evenings on the Marenport quay.

People sometimes ask what makes a small coastal restaurant worth the drive, and the honest answer has very little to do with the kitchen. It is the half-dozen people who knock on our back door before the sun is properly up: the diver, the two farmers, the cheesemaker, the forager, and the baker whose loaves are still warm when they arrive. Maison Lumière is only ever as good as their morning, and after fifteen years, their mornings are very good indeed.

The diver and the bay

Our scallops come from a single diver who has worked the bay for longer than we have been open. He hand-selects each one, takes only what the season can spare, and leaves the small ones to grow. The result is a shellfish so fresh it barely needs heat - and a supply that has never once been over-fished. We pay more for them than a wholesaler would charge, and we consider it the best money the restaurant spends.

That relationship is also why our menu can vanish on a bad-weather day. If the diver stays ashore, the scallop course comes off the card without apology. We would rather change the menu than serve something that travelled too far to reach us.

Two farms and a terrace

Most of our vegetables come from two families who farm the hills above Marenport. One grows heritage tomatoes, stone fruit and squash; the other keeps a market garden of roots, alliums and brassicas through the colder months. Between them they cover almost the entire year, and we plan our seasons around what they are about to harvest rather than what we happen to fancy.

The most delicate things - soft herbs, edible flowers, the first peas - we grow ourselves on the terrace above the dining room. It is a small plot, but it means a sprig of chervil can travel from soil to plate in minutes, and there is no substitute for that kind of freshness.

Why we stay loyal

It would be cheaper and easier to buy from a single distributor, and the food would be perfectly fine. But fine is not why anyone drives to the end of a quay for dinner. The trust we have built with these growers and gatherers shows up in every dish: in produce picked that morning, in catch handled with care, in the small generosities - an extra crate of figs, the season's first asparagus held back just for us - that no contract could ever buy.

So when you taste something here that seems to carry the place itself, that is what you are tasting. Not a technique, but a network of people who have decided, over fifteen years, to be very good to one small restaurant on the water.
